On March 18, 2020 a magnitude 5.7 earthquake occurred near Magna, UT

  • This is the largest earthquake occurring in Utah to be recorded on seismic instruments.
  • There were no serious injuries or fatalities, but estimated damage is greater than $150 million.
  • It occurred on the Salt Lake City segment of the Wasatch Fault.

Earthquakes are a serious hazard along the Wasatch Fault and it is important to have a general understanding of earthquake science.

What does an earthquake feel like?

  • Earthquakes generally get weaker the farther away from the source you are
  • Intensity is used to describe local shaking and it is determined by damage and experiences of those who felt it.
  • The most common scale is the Modified Mercalli Intensity scale, which ranges from category I to category XII. Here are some examples:
    • I - Not felt - Not felt except by very few under the right conditions.
    • V - Moderate - Felt by most. Some dishes and windows are broken.
    • VIII - Severe - Considerable damage to ordinary buildings. Fall of chimneys and factory stacks. Heavy furniture overturned.
    • IX - Violent - Considerable damage to specially designed buildings. Liquefaction occurs.
    • XI - Catastrophe - Few, if any, structures remain standing.
    • XII - Enormous Catastrophe - Damage is total.
  • Look at the intensity recorded by the different seismometers (triangles) operated by the University of Utah Seismograph Stations by hovering over them!

Magnitudes are a way to describe how big an earthquake is. They generally decrease with time.

  • Magnitude is a logarithmic scale! This means that a magnitude 2 earthquake is ten times as powerful as a magnitude 1 earthquake [cite], and a magnitude 3 is ten times more powerful than a magnitude 2. So while the difference between 2 and 5.7 might not seem like that much, a magnitude 5.7 can shake buildings miles away, while a magnitude 2 can barely be felt at the same distance.
  • Things aren't so simple though: it is possible for an earthquake to have several different magnitude estimates depending on the waves and frequencies used in the calculation [cite].
  • The largest earthquake ever recorded was a magnitude 9.5 [cite]! This earthquake had a rupture area that was around 1000 km long and 150 km wide, though. Since a fault line that big doesn't exist in Utah, and since earthquake magnitude is limited by the area available for rupture, Utah won't be seeing an earthquake on that magnitude for quite some time [cite].
